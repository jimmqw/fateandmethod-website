01 · What Are the Ten Gods?

In BaZi (八字, "Eight Characters"), every stem and branch in your chart is classified by its interaction with other elements. These interaction types are called the Ten Gods (十神) — ten archetypal forces that describe the nature of relationships between the five elements.

The Ten Gods arise from the Day Master — your chart's core element, determined by your birth date and time. Each of the ten heavenly stems interacts with the Day Master through one of these ten relationship types. Rather than good or bad, each carries a distinct energy: nurturing, challenging, supportive, draining, or balancing.

Think of the Ten Gods as a vocabulary. "Officer" describes a force of authority and discipline. "Wealth" describes a force of material resources and欲望. "Resource" describes a force of knowledge and protection. Each one can manifest positively or negatively depending on its strength, balance, and context within the chart.

The ten gods are organized into five complementary pairs — one for each element combination — and each pair contains a Zheng (正, "correct" or "primary") version and a Pian (偏, "partial" or "branch") version. Together, they form a complete system for reading the relational dynamics within any BaZi chart.

02 · Yin and Yang: Zheng vs. Pian

The core distinction between the two members of each pair is the Yin-Yang attribute of the element doing the interacting. This matters because Yin and Yang versions of the same element feel different in practice.

Zheng (正) means "correct" or "primary." It refers to the direct, matching relationship — the standard interaction between two elements of the same polarity. Zheng relationships tend to be structured, conventional, and socially recognized. A Zheng spouse, for instance, represents a formal marriage partner.

Yin Elements

In the BaZi system, Yin versions of each element carry a more subtle, internal, receptive quality. Yin earth is stable and conserving; Yin metal is refined and meticulous; Yin water is still and deeply introspective; Yin wood is flexible and artistic; Yin fire is warm but contained. When a Yin element interacts with the Day Master, it produces the Pian (偏) version of the god.

Yang Elements

Yang versions are active, external, and assertive. Yang fire burns bright and outward; Yang wood grows upward aggressively; Yang earth is expansive and solid; Yang metal is sharp and cutting; Yang water flows dynamically. When a Yang element interacts with the Day Master, it produces the Zheng (正) version of the god.

However, the Zheng/Pian distinction isn't just about Yin vs. Yang of the interacting element. It's also about the relational mode. In some pairs, the interaction involves direct generation (相生), while in others it involves indirect or partial interaction. Each of the five pairs occupies a different position in the interaction cycle, which is why they carry such distinct flavors.

03 · The Five Pairs: Zheng (Primary)

Zheng Yin — Resource
正印 · 印
Generated by the element that creates your Day Master. If you are a Wood day master, Water is your Zheng Yin. It is the force of protection, education, tradition, and inner wisdom. Zheng Yin represents a motherly, unconditional support — study, credentials, mentors, and legal documentation. It suppresses direct conflict and brings calm stability.
Supportive, protective, scholarly, conventional
Zheng Guan — Officer
正官 · 官
The element that your Day Master generates (produces). If you are a Wood day master, Metal is your Zheng Officer. It represents authority, law, regulations, and social order. Zheng Officer is a gentle but firm push toward responsibility and reputation. It governs your relationship with bosses, government, and structured institutions. In a balanced chart it grants leadership; in excess it creates fear and pressure.
Authoritative, disciplined, prestigious, cautious
Zheng Cai — Wealth
正财 · 财
The element that your Day Master overcomes (controls). If you are a Wood day master, Earth is your Zheng Cai. It represents tangible wealth — cash, property, salary, material assets earned through direct effort. Zheng Cai is a hard-earned, conventional income. It is steady, measurable, and tied to discipline and routine. Excess Zheng Cai can make a person overly materialistic or work-obsessed.
Practical, material, diligent, possessive
Zheng Shi — Food
正食 · 食
The element that your Day Master generates and which then generates something else in a flowing cycle. This is the most creative and expressive god. It represents talent, speech, joy, artistry, and the ability to communicate. Zheng Shi also governs children (especially daughters) and the pleasures of life. A strong Zheng Shi gives eloquence and innovation; an imbalanced one can lead to recklessness.
Creative, expressive, joyful, articulate
Zheng Jie — Peer (Rob)
正劫 · 劫
The same element as your Day Master but of opposite polarity. If your Day Master is Yang Wood (Jia), then Yin Wood (Yi) is your Zheng Jie. It represents peers, siblings, and companions — people who share your space and resources. Zheng Jie has a competitive quality: it drains your resources in the same way another person of your kind would. It can represent romantic rivals, business partners, or community. The key is whether the relationship is cooperative or draining.
Competitive, social, communal, draining

04 · The Five Pairs: Pian (Partial)

Pian Yin — Shadow Resource
偏印 · 枭
The Yin version of the element that creates your Day Master. Pian Yin is the same elemental generation as Zheng Yin but filtered through the Yin mode. This makes it less warm and more intellectual, even cold. It represents unorthodox knowledge, unconventional education, intuition, and spiritual insight. Pian Yin is often called "Owl" (枭) because it tends to "eat" its counterpart, Zheng Shi. It can bring depth and originality, but also obsession and isolation.
Intuitive, unconventional, spiritual, possessive
Pian Guan — Seven Killing Officer
偏官 · 七杀
The Yin version of the element your Day Master generates. Known as the Seven Killing Sha (七杀), this is the most intense and challenging of all Ten Gods. Pian Guan is direct pressure, crisis, and competition — often arriving abruptly. In small doses it drives ambition and sharpens focus. In excess it creates fear, conflict, and instability. It is the force of sudden change, powerful enemies, and transformative challenge.
Intense, challenging, transformative, aggressive
Pian Cai — Partial Wealth
偏财 · 财
The Yin version of the element your Day Master controls. Pian Cai is wealth through speculation, investment, business, or windfall — money that flows through channels less tied to fixed salary. It is associated with entrepreneurship, the stock market, gambling, and high-risk opportunity. Pian Cai is also the god of romantic relationships (for men), representing desire, attraction, and passion. It is dynamic, unpredictable, and externally focused.
Opportunistic, speculative, passionate, restless
Pian Shang — Injuring Officer
偏伤 · 伤官
The Yin counterpart to Zheng Shi. Pian Shang (sometimes called Shang Guan, "Hurting Officer") is an intensely creative force that tends to rebel against authority. Where Zheng Shi expresses through established channels, Pian Shang breaks them. It gives brilliance, unconventional genius, and sharp intellect — but also sarcasm, defiance, and a tendency to wound. Pian Shang in balance creates artists and revolutionaries; in excess it destroys careers and relationships.
Rebellious, brilliant, provocative, subversive
Bi — Parallel (Same)
比肩 · 比
The Yang counterpart to your Day Master — the same element and same polarity. Where Zheng Jie is your Yin peer, Bi is your Yang peer. Bi represents friends, colleagues, and partners who are on your wavelength — people who think and act similarly to you. Bi provides solidarity and confidence in group settings. It is the energy of the team, the alliance, and the equals. Too much Bi, however, and you become reliant on others' validation, or locked in stubborn rivalry.
Solidaristic, confident, cooperative, stubborn

05 · How to Read Ten Gods in a Chart

Reading the Ten Gods begins with identifying your Day Master and then classifying every other stem and branch in relation to it. Here is a step-by-step process:

  1. Find the Day Master — Look at the Year pillar's heavenly stem. The ten stems are: Jia (甲, Yang Wood), Yi (乙, Yin Wood), Bing (丙, Yang Fire), Ding (丁, Yin Fire), Wu (戊, Yang Earth), Ji (己, Yin Earth), Geng (庚, Yang Metal), Xin (辛, Yin Metal), Ren (壬, Yang Water), Gui (癸, Yin Water).
  2. Identify the five element roles — For your Day Master, determine which element it generates (Officer), which generates it (Resource), which it controls (Wealth), which controls it (Food), and which is its same-element peer (Bi/Jie). Each of these five becomes a "Zheng" version.
  3. Apply Yin/Yang polarity — For each relationship, check whether the interacting element is Yin or Yang. If it matches the Yin/Yang polarity of the standard interaction cycle, you get the Zheng version; if it's the opposite polarity, you get the Pian version.
  4. Count appearances — Count how many times each Ten God appears across the four pillars. The more a god appears, the more dominant that energy is in your life. A chart with three Pian Guan (Seven Killing) is a chart shaped by challenge and crisis.
  5. Assess balance — Evaluate whether the chart has too much of one god or too little of another. A chart with no Officer (官) at all will struggle with authority and structure. A chart with no Wealth (财) may have a detached relationship with material resources.
  6. Consider pillar strength — A god appearing in the Day pillar (closest to the self) has more direct personal influence than one appearing in the Year pillar. The Month pillar often shows the most active, current-life dynamics.

07 · Conclusion

The Ten Gods are not labels — they are relational forces. Each one describes how a particular element behaves toward your Day Master, and by extension, how that energy shows up in your life: the authority figures you attract, the wealth paths available to you, the creative or destructive tendencies you carry, the peers who shape you, and the resources that sustain or challenge you.

Mastering the Ten Gods is less about memorization and more about developing an intuitive feel for elemental relationships. Once you can see a chart and immediately sense that a Seven Killing Officer is pressing hard against the Day Master, or that a chart is dominated by Wealth energy with little Resource to ground it, you have internalized the language.

The practical value is this: the Ten Gods reveal where your chart is strong, where it is weak, and where it is tense. A strong Officer structure indicates leadership potential. A weak Resource line suggests the need to seek external knowledge and support. A dominant Wealth energy points to material focus — and the risk of becoming defined by possessions rather than purpose.

Study the Ten Gods not as separate symbols, but as a living system of relationships. In every BaZi chart, they are already speaking. Your next step is to learn how to identify which gods are prominent in your own chart, and what that means for your strengths, challenges, and life path.
