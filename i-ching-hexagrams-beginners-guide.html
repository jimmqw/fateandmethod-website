The Book of Changes

I Ching Hexagrams
A Complete Beginner's Guide

For over three thousand years, the I Ching has served as China's most profound oracle — guiding emperors, scholars, and seekers through its 64 hexagrams. Here is how to begin your own dialogue with the Book of Changes.

Home / Wisdom / I Ching Hexagrams Beginner's Guide

The I Ching (易经), or Yijing, known in English as the Book of Changes, is the oldest of the Chinese classics — predating Confucius, predating the Dao De Jing, predating almost every text that shaped Chinese civilization. At its core, it is an oracle built from 64 hexagrams, each a stack of six lines — some solid (yang), some broken (yin) — that together form a symbolic answer to any question life may pose.

Unlike fortune-telling systems that claim to predict fixed futures, the I Ching is fundamentally a tool for wisdom. It does not tell you what will happen; it describes the pattern you are standing in and suggests how to move through it with clarity. Kings used it to guide kingdoms. Scholars used it to cultivate character. And today, anyone can use it — with nothing more than three coins and a willingness to listen.

The Building Blocks: Yin and Yang Lines

Everything in the I Ching begins with two lines — the simplest possible expression of duality.

The Two Lines
Foundation · Duality · Change

The yang line (⚊) is a single unbroken stroke. It represents the active, creative, expansive principle — light, heat, movement, assertion, the masculine force. The yin line (⚋) is a broken stroke, a gap in the middle. It represents the receptive, yielding, contractive principle — darkness, coolness, stillness, receptivity, the feminine force. Neither is superior. The entire I Ching is a meditation on how these two forces interact, alternate, and transform into one another.

When you cast the I Ching, you generate six of these lines — one at a time, from bottom to top. Each line can appear as either stable (unchanging yin or yang) or changing (a yang that moves toward yin, or a yin that moves toward yang). Changing lines are the beating heart of the oracle — they are where the old pattern dissolves and the new one emerges.

Yang Line (⚊)

Solid, unbroken. Represents action, light, creativity, the assertive principle. Number value: 7 (stable) or 9 (changing).

Yin Line (⚋)

Broken at center. Represents receptivity, darkness, nurturing, the yielding principle. Number value: 8 (stable) or 6 (changing).

The Eight Trigrams: Ba Gua

Before there were hexagrams, there were trigrams — eight three-line figures called the Ba Gua (八卦), each representing a fundamental force of nature.

The Primordial Eight
Heaven · Earth · Thunder · Water · Mountain · Wind · Fire · Lake

Legend attributes the trigrams to Fu Xi (伏羲), the mythical first emperor of China, who saw them reflected in the patterns on the back of a dragon-horse emerging from the Yellow River. Each trigram is a unique combination of three yin/yang lines, and each carries a dense cluster of associations — a natural element, a family member, a direction, a season, an animal, a body part, a virtue.

A hexagram is simply two trigrams stacked together — a lower trigram (the inner situation) and an upper trigram (the outer situation). The 64 possible combinations of lower and upper trigrams produce the complete set of hexagrams. Understanding the trigrams is the fastest route to interpreting any hexagram you receive.

☰ Qian — Heaven

Three solid lines. Pure yang. The creative, the father, strength, initiative. South, late autumn.

☷ Kun — Earth

Three broken lines. Pure yin. The receptive, the mother, devotion, nourishment. North, early autumn.

☳ Zhen — Thunder

Yang below, yin above. The arousing. Sudden change, the eldest son, movement, growth. Northeast, spring.

☵ Kan — Water

Yin, yang, yin. The abysmal. Danger, the middle son, depth, adaptability. West, midwinter.

The 64 Hexagrams

When the eight trigrams are paired in every possible combination — upper and lower — the result is sixty-four hexagrams, each a complete six-line figure with its own name, image, judgment, and line texts.

How a Hexagram Works
Structure · Judgment · Image · Lines

Every hexagram in the I Ching comes with a Judgment (卦辞, guaci) — a concise, often poetic statement attributed to King Wen of Zhou that captures the hexagram's essential meaning — and an Image (象传, xiangzhuan), traditionally attributed to Confucius, which draws moral and practical lessons from the hexagram's structure.

Below the Judgment and Image, each of the six individual lines has its own short text. When you cast a reading, you pay special attention to any changing lines — those are the lines whose texts carry the most precise guidance for your situation. After receiving a hexagram with changing lines, you also derive a second hexagram — the one that results from flipping all changing lines to their opposites. This second hexagram represents the situation you are moving toward.

Hexagram 1: Qian — The Creative

Six solid yang lines. Pure creative force. "The Creative works sublime success, furthering through perseverance."

Hexagram 2: Kun — The Receptive

Six broken yin lines. Pure receptivity. "The Receptive brings about sublime success, furthering through the perseverance of a mare."

Hexagram 11: Tai — Peace

Heaven below, Earth above. Harmony — the strong below supports the yielding above. Prosperity and free flow.

Hexagram 64: Wei Ji — Before Completion

Fire above, Water below. The final hexagram. Things are not yet settled — caution and careful ordering are needed.

Key insight: The 64 hexagrams do not progress in a simple linear fashion from 1 to 64. The sequence itself — known as the King Wen Sequence — is a profound arrangement where each hexagram transforms into the next through inversion, polarity, or thematic contrast. Hexagram 63 (After Completion) and Hexagram 64 (Before Completion) end the sequence — one representing settled order, the other representing the eternal edge of becoming. Nothing is ever truly finished.

How to Cast the I Ching: The Three Coins Method

The most accessible way to consult the I Ching is the three coins method. You need three identical coins, a pen, paper, and a quiet mind.

The Three Coins Method
Step by Step · Casting · Recording

Decide which side of the coin represents yang (heads) with a value of 3, and which represents yin (tails) with a value of 2. Hold the coins loosely in your hands. Focus on your question — make it clear, specific, and open-ended (avoid yes/no questions; the I Ching answers "what is this situation and how should I move within it," not "should I do X or Y"). When you feel settled, toss all three coins together.

Add the values of the three coins to get a total: 6, 7, 8, or 9. This total determines the first line (bottom of the hexagram). Repeat this process five more times, building the hexagram from the bottom up — line 1 at the base, line 6 at the top. Record each result carefully.

6 — Old Yin (⚋→⚊)

Three tails (2+2+2). A changing yin line that transforms into yang. Draw as a broken line with a cross or circle in the gap.

7 — Young Yang (⚊)

Two heads, one tail (3+3+2). A stable yang line. Draw as a solid line. Does not change.

8 — Young Yin (⚋)

Two tails, one head (2+2+3). A stable yin line. Draw as a broken line. Does not change.

9 — Old Yang (⚊→⚋)

Three heads (3+3+3). A changing yang line that transforms into yin. Draw as a solid line with a circle.

Casting tip: Before you begin, write your question down clearly. The I Ching responds to the focus you bring. If your mind is scattered, take a few deep breaths. If you receive no changing lines (all 7s and 8s), the hexagram's Judgment and Image alone are your guide — the situation is stable. If you receive one or more changing lines (6s and 9s), read those line texts first; they carry the core message. Then, flip those lines to derive your approaching hexagram — the pattern you are evolving into.

How to Interpret Your Reading

Interpretation is where the I Ching shifts from a mechanical process to an art form. Here is a practical framework.

The Interpretation Framework
Four Steps · Reading · Reflection

Step 1 — Identify the hexagram. Using a reference (Wilhelm/Baynes or Huang translation recommended), find your hexagram by its lower and upper trigrams. Read the Judgment and the Image. Sit with them. The I Ching speaks in symbols and analogies; do not expect literal instructions. Expect resonance.

Step 2 — Read changing line texts. If you cast one or more changing lines, read the text for each of those lines. These are the most specific, actionable parts of the reading. If you have multiple changing lines, the lowest-numbered changing line is traditionally given the most weight.

Step 3 — Look at the approaching hexagram. Flip all changing lines. The resulting hexagram describes the situation you are moving toward — the pattern that emerges after the changes you are currently navigating. Read its Judgment for context on the direction of things.

Step 4 — Reflect, do not obsess. The I Ching rewards those who read once, absorb the message, and then go live their lives. Repeated casting on the same question — trying to force a preferred answer — is discouraged in all classical traditions. Trust the first response.

The Judgment

The hexagram's title and core meaning. Read this first — it sets the entire field. What pattern does this hexagram name?

The Image

Confucian commentary on the hexagram's symbolism. Often gives moral or strategic counsel. "Thus the superior person…"

Changing Lines

The specific lines that are 6 or 9. These pinpoint where the action is — the exact pressure point in your situation.

Approaching Hexagram

The hexagram after flipping changing lines. Represents the emerging pattern. Use it for orientation, not as a fixed destiny.

The I Ching in Modern Life

For the modern practitioner, the I Ching offers something increasingly rare: a structured pause. In a world of instant answers and algorithmic recommendations, the act of sitting down with three coins, formulating a genuine question, and waiting for a pattern to emerge is itself a countercultural practice.

Psychologists — most notably Carl Jung — have found in the I Ching a working model of what Jung called synchronicity: meaningful coincidence that is not causal but acausal, a resonance between inner question and outer event. Whether you understand this as wisdom, as psychology, or as genuine divination, the I Ching functions. It does not require belief. It requires engagement.

Begin with one hexagram a week. Write it down. Reflect on it. Over months, the 64 hexagrams become not just symbols in a book but a vocabulary for the patterns of your own life — and that, perhaps, is exactly what they were always meant to become.

Essential translations: For English readers, the Wilhelm/Baynes translation (with Carl Jung's foreword) remains the gold standard for serious study. Alfred Huang's The Complete I Ching offers a modern, accessible alternative with excellent trigram analysis. Avoid abridged or "fortune telling" adaptations — the full text rewards patience.
