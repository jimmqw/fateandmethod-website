How to Cast and Read I Ching Coins — A Complete Liu Yao Beginner's Guide

Everything you need to perform your first Liu Yao divination: from choosing coins to interpreting changing hexagrams. No prior knowledge required.

Liu Yao is the most practical branch of the I Ching (Yijing) — the Book of Changes. While the original text requires deep philosophical study, Liu Yao reduces the system to a clear, repeatable method: you ask a specific question, cast three coins six times, build a hexagram from the bottom up, and interpret the relationships between the six lines. This guide takes you from zero to your first complete reading in under an hour.

01 · What Is Liu Yao?

Liu Yao (六爻) literally means "Six Lines." It's a divination method derived from the I Ching that uses three coins tossed six times to generate a six-line hexagram. Each toss produces one line, and the pattern of broken and solid lines — plus whether any lines are "changing" — determines the reading.

Unlike the classical I Ching text (which relies on memorizing 64 hexagram judgments written in archaic poetry), Liu Yao focuses on line relationships: which line represents you (the Self line, 世爻), which line represents the subject of your question (the Other line, 应爻), and how the Five Elements interact between them. This makes Liu Yao remarkably specific — you can ask about a job offer, a relationship, a financial decision, or the timing of an event, and the system produces a concrete answer.

Liu Yao traces its modern form to the Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE) scholar Jing Fang (京房), who integrated the Five Elements, the Chinese calendar (Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches), and the hexagram structure into a unified predictive framework. For two thousand years, it remained one of the most trusted divination tools among Chinese scholars, officials, and merchants.

02 · What You Need to Get Started

You need exactly three identical coins. Tradition calls for Chinese bronze cash coins with a square hole, but any three identical coins work equally well — quarters, euros, rupees, or yen. The only requirement is that you can distinguish heads from tails.

Coin Sides and Their Values

In modern Liu Yao practice:

  • Heads = Yang (solid, value of 3)
  • Tails = Yin (broken, value of 2)

Each toss produces a total between 6 and 9, which determines the line type.

Besides coins, you need nothing else — though a pen and paper to record your results as you cast is helpful. There is no altar, no incense, no special clothing required. The system works because of its mathematical structure, not ritual.

03 · How to Cast the Coins

The Six Steps of Casting

  1. Formulate a clear, specific question. "Will I get the job at Acme Corp?" is good. "What will my life be like?" is too vague.
  2. Hold the three coins in your hands. Focus your mind on the question for 10–15 seconds.
  3. Toss all three coins simultaneously onto a flat surface.
  4. Count the heads and tails and calculate the total value (each head = 3, each tail = 2).
  5. Record the line as described in the table below. This is Line 1 (the bottom line).
  6. Repeat five more times, recording each new line above the previous ones, building the hexagram from bottom to top.

309Old Yang⚊ (changing)Solid line that changes to broken
218Young Yin⚋ (stable)Broken line, unchanging

036Old Yin⚋ (changing)Broken line that changes to solid

The critical distinction: lines totaling 7 or 8 are stable (they stay as they are). Lines totaling 6 or 9 are changing (they flip to their opposite). A changing line is the most important feature in any reading — it's where the action is.

04 · Building the Hexagram

Once you've cast all six lines, you have two hexagrams:

1. The Original Hexagram (本卦) — all six lines as originally cast, ignoring changes.
2. The Transformed Hexagram (变卦) — the original hexagram with all changing lines flipped to their opposites.

Example Cast

Suppose you cast these six lines (bottom to top):

  • Line 1: 2 heads, 1 tail = 8 → Young Yin (⚋ stable)
  • Line 2: 3 heads = 9 → Old Yang (⚊ changing to ⚋)
  • Line 3: 1 head, 2 tails = 7 → Young Yang (⚊ stable)
  • Line 4: 1 head, 2 tails = 7 → Young Yang (⚊ stable)
  • Line 5: 0 heads, 3 tails = 6 → Old Yin (⚋ changing to ⚊)
  • Line 6: 2 heads, 1 tail = 8 → Young Yin (⚋ stable)

Original Hexagram: Yin-Yang-Yang-Yang-Yin-Yin (䷾ — Hexagram 63, Ji Ji / "Already Fording")
Transformed Hexagram: Yin-Yin-Yang-Yang-Yang-Yin (䷤ — Hexagram 37, Jia Ren / "The Family")

The relationship between the Original and Transformed hexagrams tells the story of your situation: where you start and where you're heading. Lines 2 and 5 are changing, so those two positions carry the most weight in the interpretation.

05 · Self and Other Lines: The Core of Interpretation

Every hexagram has a designated Self line (世爻) and Other line (应爻), fixed by tradition. The Self line represents you, the questioner. The Other line represents the subject of your question — whether it's a person, a job, an investment, or an event.

The relationship between the Self and Other lines is the single most important factor in any Liu Yao reading:

RelationshipMeaning
Other generates Self (水生木)The situation supports and benefits you — excellent
Self generates Other (木生火)You are pouring energy into the situation — draining
Other controls Self (金克木)The situation opposes or threatens you — caution needed
Self controls Other (木克土)You have control over the situation — but effort required
Self and Other are the same elementHarmony and mutual understanding — favorable for relationships

For example, if you ask about a job interview and the Other line's element generates the Self line's element (say, Water generating Wood), the reading strongly favors you — the company or interviewer will be receptive. If the Other line controls the Self line, prepare for a challenging interaction.

06 · Interpreting Changing Lines

Changing lines (Old Yang and Old Yin) are where the divination's energy concentrates. They represent the dynamic aspect of your situation — what's moving, what's unstable, what's transforming.

Rule of thumb: If only one line is changing, that line is the answer. If multiple lines change, the topmost changing line carries the primary message, and lower changing lines provide supporting detail.

When a line changes from Yang to Yin, it typically signals a shift from active to passive — perhaps you should stop pushing and wait. When a line changes from Yin to Yang, it signals a shift from passive to active — it's time to act.

The position of the changing line also matters:

  • Line 1 (bottom): The foundation — changing circumstances at the root level
  • Lines 2–3: Personal sphere — internal shifts in your own situation
  • Lines 4–5: External sphere — shifts involving others, career, public life
  • Line 6 (top): The outcome or culmination — the final state toward which things are moving

07 · Timing Your Question with the Calendar

Advanced Liu Yao practitioners layer the Chinese calendar (Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches) onto each line, which provides precise timing information. The day and month of your consultation assign an element to each of the six lines, and the Five Element cycles tell you when in the calendar a predicted event is likely to occur.

As a beginner, you don't need to memorize the sexagenary cycle. Start with two fundamentals:

  • The Month of the divination determines which element is strongest — this affects which lines in your hexagram are "supported" or "weakened."
  • The Day of the divination interacts with each line — a line that matches the day's element is especially potent.

For your first few dozen readings, focus on the coin casting, hexagram construction, and Self/Other line relationships. The calendar layer adds precision but is not required for accurate readings — the core logic of line relationships alone produces reliable results.

08 · Your First Practice Reading

Here's a complete example to walk through:

Sample Question: "Should I accept the job offer from Company X?"

1. Cast the coins: Generate your six lines and build the original and transformed hexagrams.

2. Identify the Self and Other lines: Look up your hexagram number in any Liu Yao reference (or the I Ching) to find which line position is the Self line. The Other line is always exactly opposite (if Self is Line 3, Other is Line 6; if Self is Line 2, Other is Line 5).

3. Check the Five Element relationship: If the Self line is Wood and the Other line is Water → Water generates Wood → the job is supportive of you. If Self is Wood and Other is Metal → Metal controls Wood → be cautious.

4. Read any changing lines: If the Self line itself is changing, you are the one transforming through this situation. If the Other line is changing, the job itself (or the company) is the unstable factor.

5. Synthesize: Combine all observations into a clear answer. "The job's element supports you (favorable), but the Self line is changing Old Yang → you may discover this role transforms you in unexpected ways."

09 · Conclusion

Liu Yao rewards practice more than study. The theoretical framework — hexagrams, changing lines, Five Elements, the calendar — can be learned in a weekend. But the intuitive feel for what a particular combination means only develops through repeated casting and honest reflection on the results.

The beauty of Liu Yao is that the system has no ambiguity about its own rules. Three coins, six tosses, a clear question, and a rigorously defined interpretation framework produce answers that are, more often than not, startlingly accurate. The ancient Chinese called this 感应 (gan ying) — "resonance" — the idea that when you sincerely focus on a question, the universe responds through the pattern of the coins.

Your next step: cast for something small and verifiable — not a life-changing decision, but a question you'll know the answer to within days or weeks. Track your results. The accuracy you observe will tell you more than any guide ever could.
