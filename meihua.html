Mei Hua Yi Shu (жў…иҠұжҳ“ж•°) — Plum Blossom Numerology — is the most spontaneous and creatively flexible divination system in Chinese metaphysics. Attributed to the Song Dynasty master Shao Yong (йӮөйӣҚ), it can use ANYTHING as divination data: the time on a clock when you walk in, the shape of a cloud, the sound of a car horn, the number of people walking past. The readings are notoriously fast and scarily accurate.

What is Mei Hua Yi Shu?

While most divination systems require a formal ritual — coins, yarrow stalks, a compass — Mei Hua Yi Shu liberated the practice entirely. Shao Yong’s insight was profound: if everything in the universe is connected through number, then anything can be a valid data point. The universe doesn’t need you to throw coins; it’s already speaking in the moment you observe.

The system is grounded in the principles of the I Ching but extends them into a kind of phenomenological divination — where the practitioner treats any spontaneous observation as raw data. A businessman walks into a room and glances at a clock showing 3:47. A Mei Hua reader immediately reads this as the hexagram for the situation — 3 for the lower trigram, 47 for the upper trigram through a specific mathematical mapping.

The genius of Shao Yong was recognizing that the moment of observation is itself meaningful. Why you looked at that clock at exactly that moment — what question was forming in your mind — is not separate from the time it showed. The numbers and the observer are one system.

The Principle: Numbers as the Language of the Universe

Shao Yong believed that the universe expresses itself through number. Every phenomenon — every sound, shape, color, and moment — carries a numerical signature. The task of the Mei Hua practitioner is to recognize these signatures and map them onto the 64 hexagrams of the I Ching.

The conversion principle: divide any quantity or observation into two parts — the “upper number” and the “lower number” — which map to the upper and lower trigrams of a hexagram. From the hexagram, the full interpretive apparatus of the I Ching becomes available.

What makes Mei Hua unique is that it does not generate numbers through ritual — it extracts them from the phenomenal world. The reading is therefore inseparable from the context of the moment: who you were with, what you were doing, what prompted you to ask the question in the first place.

Spontaneous Method: Any Phenomena as Data

The spontaneous method is the defining feature of Mei Hua Yi Shu. Practitioners are trained to treat any salient observation as potential divination data.

Observation Method (еӨ–еә”)

Any spontaneous external event observed at the moment of question becomes the reading. A bird flies past at an odd angle — this modifies the hexagram. A sudden loud noise — this is an omen. A person entering the room wearing a specific color — this is data.

The number of sounds heard within a time window — a car horn, a door closing, someone laughing — each sound can be counted and mapped to a hexagram. Practitioners report this method as particularly reliable for questions about timing.

Shape Method (еҪўиұЎ)

The shape of an object encountered spontaneously — a bent tree branch, a broken cup, a perfectly round stone — each carries symbolic weight that maps to a hexagram archetype within the Mei Hua framework.

Social Method (дәәдәӢ)

The gender, age, and number of people encountered at the moment of question — a group of three young men, a single elderly woman — each variable feeds into the calculation.

Time-Based Casting (еҲҶй’ҹиө·еҚҰ)

The most systematic Mei Hua method uses the time of the question as the primary data. The current hour, minute, and second are converted into a hexagram through a direct mathematical mapping.

Example: A question arises at 10:15. The hour 10 becomes the lower trigram (using a specific correspondence table). The minute 15 — treated as 1 + 5 = 6 — becomes the upper trigram. The resulting hexagram is read in the standard I Ching manner.

This method is favored for questions where no external phenomena present themselves — when you simply realize you need guidance and want to cast a reading immediately using the clock.

Classic story: Shao Yong himself reportedly used Mei Hua to predict that a certain stone bridge would see a young boy fall into the water at a specific hour — and sent his disciples to prevent it. When they arrived, they found the boy had already fallen but was rescued. The prediction was both accurate and “overridden” through foreknowledge — a paradox that illustrates the Mei Hua view of fate as fluid, not fixed.

Once a hexagram is derived, it can be placed within the Nine Palace Grid (Lo Shu square) to extend the reading. This adds a spatial dimension — the question is not only what but also where. Different palaces of the Nine Palace grid represent different life domains, and the hexagram placed within each palace modifies the reading accordingly.

The Nine Palace extension is particularly powerful for questions about location — where should I go, where will I find it, which direction should I face. Mei Hua readers often carry a Nine Palace compass to supplement their time-based readings.

Famous Predictions in History

Shao Yong is credited with several legendary predictions, including a famous calculation of the number of stars in the heavens (which he placed at 129,600 — a number later verified by astronomical observation, though the verification method is disputed). More practically, his disciples used Mei Hua to navigate military situations, identify optimal farming times, and predict the outcomes of political negotiations.

The system remained primarily in scholarly and Taoist circles for centuries. Its revival in the late Ming and Qing periods brought it to a wider audience, though accurate English-language teaching of Mei Hua Yi Shu remains nearly nonexistent to this day.
