The Complete Feng Shui Guide (椋庢按)

China's 4,000-year-old art of spatial harmony —?core principles, common mistakes, and practical room-by-room application for modern homes.

Feng shui is not interior decorating with crystals and houseplants. It is an ancient Chinese systematic framework for understanding how energy —?qi (姘? —?moves through physical space, and how that movement affects the fortunes of the people living within it.

What Feng Shui Actually Is

The term itself translates roughly as "wind-water" —?the two elements most associated with qi in classical Chinese cosmology. The tradition holds that the landscape's natural forms create channels through which qi flows, and that buildings positioned in harmony with these channels receive beneficial energy, while those positioned against them suffer stagnant or harmful qi.

This is not superstition in the pejorative sense. Classical feng shui was, among other things, an early form of environmental science. Observant practitioners noticed correlations between a building's orientation, its immediate surroundings, and the health and fortune of its occupants. These observations accumulated over centuries into a sophisticated, internally consistent system.

The Fundamental Insight

Qi flows where attention goes. Feng shui is the art of directing human attention —?through form, orientation, color, and arrangement —?to influence the energy of a space and, by extension, the life of its inhabitants.

The Five Core Principles

Before applying any feng shui technique, you need to understand the framework beneath it. These five principles form the foundation of all classical feng shui practice.

01

Qi —?The Breath of Life

Qi is the vital energy that permeates everything. In feng shui, your goal is to attract and retain beneficial qi in your living space. Stagnant qi creates misfortune; flowing qi that slows pleasantly creates abundance.

02

Yin and Yang

The complementary forces of stillness and activity. A space that is all yang —?bright, loud, chaotic —?creates anxiety. A space that is all yin —?dark, still, heavy —?creates depression. Balance is the goal.

03

The Five Elements

Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, and Water. Each element is associated with specific colors, shapes, directions, and life areas. A balanced home incorporates all five elements in proportion. Excess of any element creates imbalance.

04

Ba Gua —?The Eight Trigrams

The eight directions and their corresponding life areas: Career, Knowledge, Family, Wealth, Fame, Romance, Children, and Health. The ba gua map is overlaid on your floor plan to identify which areas of your home govern which aspects of your life.

05

cardinal Directions and Lo Shu Square

The classic feng shui compass system relates each of the nine grid squares to a direction, element, and life area. Your birth chart and your home's facing direction determine which areas are most critical for your personal feng shui.

Room-by-Room Feng Shui Guide

馃彔 The Entrance (Ming Tang)

The entrance is the mouth of the house —?where qi enters. It is the single most important feng shui area in any home.

  • Keep the entrance well-lit, clean, and free of obstruction
  • A bright light above the door welcomes beneficial qi
  • Avoid placing shoes, trash cans, or sharp objects directly inside the door
  • A small plant or water feature near the entrance encourages qi to slow and settle
  • The entrance door should open easily and not scrape the floor

馃泲锔?The Living Room

The living room is where family qi circulates. It should feel welcoming, balanced, and alive.

  • Furniture should be arranged so everyone can see the door (the "command position")
  • A sofa against a solid wall provides psychological security
  • Sharp coffee table corners create sha qi —?round or soft-edged furniture is preferable
  • Plants bring wood energy and vitality; avoid dead or dying plants
  • Televisions should not be the focal point of the room

馃嵆 The Kitchen

In classical feng shui, the kitchen is the source of the home's prosperity, as it relates to nourishment and abundance.

  • The stove should never face the kitchen entrance —?it "shoots" qi away
  • Keep the stove clean and in good repair
  • Store sharp objects (knives) in drawers, not visible on counters
  • A half-full water jar in the kitchen symbolizes ongoing abundance
  • Avoid clutter under the sink —?it's a water area and should be kept clean

馃洀锔?The Bedroom

The bedroom governs romance, health, and rest. It should prioritize stillness and yin energy above all else.

  • The bed should rest against a solid wall with a clear view of the door
  • Never place the bed directly under a window or directly aligned with the door
  • Remove mirrors from direct view of the bed —?they activate yang energy
  • Keep work desks and exercise equipment out of the bedroom
  • Soft, rounded lighting creates restful qi; avoid harsh overhead spotlights
  • Clutter under the bed disturbs rest —?keep it clear

馃捈 The Home Office

The office governs career and knowledge. It benefits from active, yang-leaning energy balanced with focus-enhancing stillness.

  • Face the door while working —?this gives you a sense of command
  • Never sit with your back to the door —?it creates "poison arrow" sha qi
  • A solid chair back supports you psychologically
  • Earth element (brown, beige, yellow) supports concentration
  • Keep the desk organized —?chaos in the work area creates chaos in career

The Most Common Feng Shui Mistakes

Clutter

Physical clutter is stagnant qi. No amount of feng shui adjustment compensates for a cluttered home.

Broken or Non-Functional Items

Broken locks, dripping faucets, and inoperable lights signal to the subconscious that abundance is leaking away.

Sharp Corners Pointing Inward

Sharp architectural corners that point into living spaces create aggressive sha qi. Use plants or soft furnishings to soften them.

Bedroom Mirrors

Mirrors facing the bed reflect and multiply energy, disturbing rest and creating anxiety.

How to Know If Your Feng Shui Is Working

The honest answer: you observe. Classical feng shui practice was built on longitudinal observation. You adjust something, and then you watch —?over weeks and months —?whether the relevant areas of your life begin to shift.

The signs of improving feng shui are typically subtle at first: better sleep, a sense of ease when entering certain rooms, fewer minor conflicts, a feeling that things are simply flowing more smoothly. Over time, more tangible shifts in career, relationships, and health become apparent.

Feng shui is not magic. It is the deliberate design of your environment to support the life you want to live —?one of the oldest and most practical forms of self-improvement that exists.
