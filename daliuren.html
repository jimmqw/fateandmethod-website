While Liu Yao derives from the I Ching and Xuan Kong from Qi energy, Da Liu Ren operates on a day-based celestial stem and earthly branch system. Used in imperial China for state-level decisions, military strategy, and critical undertakings, it is renowned for extraordinary accuracy in timing and direction.

What is Da Liu Ren?

Da Liu Ren is built on a simple but powerful premise: every day has a unique energetic signature, determined by the combination of one of the Ten Celestial Stems (еҚҒеӨ©е№? and one of the Twelve Earthly Branches (еҚҒдәҢең°ж”Ҝ). These 60 combinations — the same 60-year cycle used in the Chinese calendar — each carry a distinct energy that shapes what can and cannot succeed on that day.

The system maps these daily combinations onto a framework of 12 palaces (similar in concept to the Zi Wei palaces, though structurally distinct), and reads the interactions between them to produce timing guidance and directional advice.

What makes Da Liu Ren distinct from Liu Yao is its day-basis: the primary variable is the day of the reading, not the moment. This makes it particularly suited to questions where the timing is spread over days or weeks, rather than a single immediate moment.

The Imperial History: How It Was Used

Da Liu Ren was the divination system of choice for Chinese emperors when facing military campaigns, major state decisions, and natural disaster response. The system’s strength in timing made it indispensable: an emperor might ask “on which day should we launch this campaign?” or “which direction should our envoy travel?”

The military applications were particularly sophisticated. Generals used Da Liu Ren to assess whether the enemy’s energy was rising or falling, whether the day was favorable for engagement, and which routes to avoid. Several historical texts credit Da Liu Ren readings with turning the tide of battles.

Unlike Zi Wei which became broadly accessible over time, Da Liu Ren remained closely held within imperial and scholarly circles. Its complexity — requiring mastery of both stems and branches and their complex interactions — meant it was rarely taught outside of direct master-pupil lineages. This exclusivity is part of what makes comprehensive English-language Da Liu Ren resources so rare today.

Why Is It Called “Six Ren”?

The name “Six Ren” (е…ӯеЈ¬) refers to the fact that among the Ten Celestial Stems, Ren (еЈ?— Yang Water) is the 9th, and in the classic formulation of the system, six of the ten stems are the primary active agents in a Da Liu Ren chart. The system uses the interactions between these six stems and the twelve branches to generate its readings.

Some scholars trace the name more poetically to the idea that the system works through the “water element” (Ren is Yang Water) — water being associated with flow, adaptability, and the carrying of information — which mirrors the system’s ability to reveal what is hidden beneath the surface of a situation.
