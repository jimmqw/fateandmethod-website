Xuan Kong (еҘҮй—ЁйҒҒз”І), or Secret Gate Numerology, is perhaps the most tactical of all Chinese divination systems. Developed for military use in ancient China, it maps the optimal energy of any given moment across nine palaces, identifying favorable directions, timing windows, and strategic positions. Today it is widely applied in Feng Shui selection, business launches, and choosing the right moment to act.

What is Xuan Kong?

Xuan Kong is fundamentally a system of timing and direction. Where other divination systems describe the quality of a situation (“what will happen”), Xuan Kong goes further: it identifies when energy is most favorable and where — in which direction, in which space — you should position yourself to work with it.

The name itself reveals its military origins: Xuan (зҺ? means mysterious or dark, Men (й—? means gate, and Dun (йҒ? means to escape or conceal. Jia (з”? is the first Celestial Stem — the Yang Wood energy that initiates action. Together, the name describes a system for concealing one’s true direction (protecting the Jia energy) while exploiting the openings in the cosmic pattern.

In practical modern use, Xuan Kong is the system of choice for: choosing a wedding date, selecting an office location, determining the best direction to face during an important meeting, and launching a new product at the optimal time window.

The Eight Gates (е…«й—Ё)

The Eight Gates are the active agents in a Xuan Kong chart — they represent the type of energy available in each palace at a given time.

Sheng Men (生门)

Life Gate — The most favorable. Vitality, new beginnings, healing. Ideal for starting new ventures.

Jing Men (景门)

Scenery Gate — Visibility, recognition. Good for presentations, marketing, going public.

Du Men (欢门)

Delight Gate — Joy, gathering. Social events, celebrations, partnership formation.

Xiu Men (休门)

Rest Gate — Rest, recovery. Suitable for consolidation, not for launching.

Si Men (死门)

Death Gate — Avoid. Represents loss, termination, closure. Not for new ventures.

Jin Men (伤门)

Injury Gate — Danger, loss. Particularly unfavorable for health and finances.

Chu Men (出门)

Out Gate — Departure, external activity. Suitable for travel, relocation.

Si Men (殉门)

Calamity Gate — Misfortune, instability. The worst gate for any significant action.

The 24 Qi Sections (24иҠӮж°”)

Xuan Kong integrates the 24 Solar Terms (24иҠӮж°”) of the Chinese agricultural calendar into its timing framework. Each solar term lasts approximately 15 days and marks a shift in the dominant Qi energy of the season. Xuan Kong readings take the current solar term into account — the same hexagram or palace can have very different meanings in different seasons.

This integration is what makes Xuan Kong particularly powerful for agricultural, health, and seasonal timing decisions. A Xuan Kong practitioner knows not just what day is favorable, but what the seasonal Qi will do to that favorability — a deep layer of nuance absent in simpler systems.

Xuan Kong in Feng Shui (йҖүеқҖ)

Modern Feng Shui extensively incorporates Xuan Kong principles for site selection and directional analysis. When choosing a home or office:

  • The facing direction of a building places it in a specific Xuan Kong palace
  • The gate door location determines which of the Eight Gates governs the property
  • The time of construction or renovation determines which Nine Star configuration is active

Practitioners use Xuan Kong Flying Star charts to analyze existing buildings and identify which periods of time will be most and least favorable for the occupants — a practice called Xuan Kong Fei Xing (зҺ„з©әйЈһжҳҹ).

Business application: A company launching a new product might use Xuan Kong to select both the optimal launch date and the optimal launch location (which direction the product should face in the room, which market to target geographically). This level of tactical specificity is what sets Xuan Kong apart from more general systems.
