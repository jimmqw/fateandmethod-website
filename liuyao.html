Liu Yao (六爻)
The Art of Change Through Three Coins and Six Lines

Of all the ways to consult the I Ching, Liu Yao is the most precise. Three coins. Six throws. Sixty-four hexagrams. One honest answer.

The Yi Jing (易经 — Book of Changes) is China's oldest and most revered divination text, a work of such depth and subtlety that Confucius himself is said to have spent years studying it. Among the several methods for consulting the Yi Jing, Liu Yao (六爻 — "Six Lines") is among the most direct, the most structured, and — for those who master it — the most revealing. Where other systems like Mei Hua Yi Shu draw inspiration from the moment spontaneously, Liu Yao imposes a rigorous six-step ritual that generates its answer through the interaction of coin tosses with the five elements. The result is a precise, layered reading that can address almost any question with remarkable specificity.

What Is Liu Yao?

Liu Yao is a coin-based Yi Jing divination system in which the seeker throws three coins six times to generate a single hexagram. Each throw produces one of the I Ching's sixty-four hexagrams, composed of six lines — each line either yang (solid, unbroken) or yin (broken). The sequence of six throws creates a complete hexagram that represents the energetic situation at the time of the question.

What distinguishes Liu Yao from other Yi Jing methods — and from the traditional yarrow stalk method — is the role of the five elements in interpretation. In Liu Yao, each of the six lines is assigned to one of the five elements based on the coin result, and this elemental assignment adds a layer of dynamic analysis to the otherwise static hexagram structure. A yang line generated by three heads (coin yang) carries a different elemental signature than a yang line generated by two heads and one tail — even though both are yang lines. This hidden element (暗动) is central to how Liu Yao extracts timing, intensity, and relational nuance from the reading.

How Three Coins Generate Six Lines

The mechanics of Liu Yao are elegant in their simplicity. Three coins are held in the hand, the question is consciously focused, and the coins are thrown. Each throw is recorded as one line, from bottom to top — building the hexagram from its foundation upward.

The result of each throw is determined by counting the number of heads:

  • 3 heads — Old Yang (老阳) — a yang line that is moving (will transform into yin)
  • 2 heads + 1 tail — Young Yang (少阳) — a stable yang line
  • 2 tails + 1 head — Young Yin (少阴) — a stable yin line
  • 3 tails — Old Yin (老阴) — a yin line that is moving (will transform into yang)

The old yang and old yin lines are called moving lines (动爻) — they carry special significance because they indicate active transformation within the hexagram. When a moving line transforms, it changes the hexagram itself, creating a transformed hexagram that represents where the situation is heading, not just where it is now. This is how Liu Yao incorporates the dimension of time and change into what would otherwise be a static reading.

The 64 Hexagrams in Liu Yao

The sixty-four hexagrams of the Yi Jing are arranged in a sequence that reflects the fundamental logic of change itself. Each hexagram is composed of two trigrams (八卦) — one above, one below — and each trigram carries both an elemental nature and a set of symbolic associations that color the meaning of the hexagram.

Qian ☰

Heaven — Pure Yang, Creativity, Strength

Kun ☷

Earth — Pure Yin, Receptivity, Nourishment

Zhen ☳

Thunder — Movement, Initiative, Arousal

Xun ☴

Wind — Penetration, Gentleness, Distribution

Kan ☵

Water — Danger, Abyss, Intelligence

Li ☲

Fire — Clarity, Adorning,依附

Gen ☶

Mountain — Stillness, Rest, Steadfastness

Dui ☱

Lake — Joy, Satisfaction, Celebration

The meaning of any hexagram emerges from the interaction between its upper and lower trigram, modified by which specific lines are moving. The hexagram Qian (全部六条阳爻) represents pure creative force; the hexagram Kun (全部六条阴爻) represents pure receptivity. Between these two poles lie sixty-two hexagrams of infinite subtlety — each one a distinct configuration of cosmic forces that maps precisely onto some aspect of the human situation.

The Five Elements in Each Line

What makes Liu Yao distinctively powerful among Yi Jing methods is its incorporation of the Five Elements (五行) into each line reading. Each coin result carries an elemental assignment based on the interaction between the throw result and the day stem of the reading. These elemental assignments create a secondary layer of interpretation that adds relational dynamics to the structural analysis.

When an element in one line generates (生) the element in an adjacent line, the relationship is supportive — energy flows between them. When one element overcomes (克) another, tension is created. Moving lines introduce further complexity: when an old yang line transforms, it generates a new elemental assignment that changes the relational dynamics of the entire hexagram.

Mastering the Five Elements in Liu Yao is what separates a basic hexagram reading from a sophisticated one. It allows the practitioner to identify not just the general situation described by the hexagram, but the specific energetic texture of that situation — where energy is building, where it is blocked, where it is in danger of collapsing.

"A hexagram without the Five Elements is a map without terrain. It shows you where you are, but not what the ground beneath you is made of, or whether the path ahead is river or mountain. Liu Yao adds the terrain."

Reading the Dynamic: Moving Lines and the Transformed Hexagram

The presence of one or more moving lines in a Liu Yao reading is what gives the chart its dynamic quality — the sense of a situation in motion rather than at rest. A hexagram with no moving lines describes a stable situation, one that is unlikely to change without significant external intervention. A hexagram with one moving line indicates a focused point of change that will drive the situation's evolution. Two or more moving lines create a more complex picture of simultaneous transformation in multiple areas.

The moving lines also determine the timing of developments. In Liu Yao tradition, the relative position of the moving lines within the hexagram — from first line at the bottom to sixth line at the top — provides clues about when changes will manifest. Lower moving lines tend to indicate near-term developments; higher moving lines point toward later stages of the situation's evolution.

The transformed hexagram — the new hexagram created when all moving lines have completed their transformation — represents the destination of the current trajectory. Reading both the original and the transformed hexagram simultaneously is what allows Liu Yao to describe both the present moment and where it is heading.

Eight Trigrams as Building Blocks

The eight trigrams (八卦) are the foundational vocabulary of the Yi Jing — eight three-line figures that represent the eight fundamental energies of the cosmos. They are the building blocks from which the sixty-four hexagrams are constructed, and understanding them is essential to reading Liu Yao with depth.

Each trigram has a primary element, a primary direction, a primary family member, and a primary body part. Zhen (Zhen, Thunder) is the eldest son; Xun (Wind) is the eldest daughter. Qian (Heaven) is the father; Kun (Earth) is the mother. When a trigram appears in a specific position within a hexagram — upper or lower, active or passive — its family role colors the interpretation. A question about a business partnership might be read through the lens of the father trigram (authority, structure) or the youngest son trigram (rebellion, movement), depending on where it appears in the chart.

Your Question as the Anchor

In Liu Yao, the question is not incidental — it is the anchor around which the entire reading is organized. Every hexagram can be interpreted in many ways, and it is the specificity of the question that directs the practitioner toward the relevant layer of meaning. A question about whether to accept a job offer activates different palace meanings and different line interpretations than a question about whether the same offer will lead to financial security.

The most important quality of a Liu Yao question is focus. A vague question — "what does my future look like?" — produces a vague answer. A specific question — "should I accept the role as regional director by the end of this month?" — produces a precise and useful answer. The more specific the question, the more specific the response the system is capable of delivering.

Getting Started with Three Coins

Begin Your Journey into Coin Divination

  1. Hold three coins with clear intention — Any coins of equal denomination will work. Hold them in your cupped hands, focus on your question, and shake them gently. The quality of your focus is the quality of your reading.
  2. Throw and record six times — Each throw produces one line, recorded from bottom to top. Count heads: 3 heads = old yang (moving), 2 heads + 1 tail = young yang (stable), 2 tails + 1 head = young yin (stable), 3 tails = old yin (moving). Draw each line as solid for yang, broken for yin, marking moving lines clearly.
  3. Identify the hexagram — Match your six-line figure to one of the sixty-four hexagrams. The Yi Jing text for that hexagram is your primary interpretive foundation. Pay most attention to the moving lines' passages.
  4. Apply the Five Elements to each line — Determine the elemental assignment of each line based on the throw result and the day stem. Map the generating and overcoming relationships between adjacent lines.
  5. Read the transformed hexagram — Change all moving lines to their opposite (yang becomes yin, yin becomes yang) and read the resulting hexagram as the destination of the current trajectory.
  6. Anchor the reading to your question — The hexagram means many things. Your question tells you which meaning is relevant right now. Return to the question again and again as you interpret.
